About Me

I am a Postdoctoral Researcher at Swansea University, working on magnetically controlled aligned and oriented H₂ reactive scattering on metal surfaces. My research focuses on stereodynamics, quantum-state–resolved molecule–surface interactions, and energy-transfer mechanisms in gas–surface reactions.

Research Overview

My research is broadly divided into two major areas: understanding CO₂ dissociation on metal surfaces, which is directly relevant to heterogeneous catalysis and global warming, and the development of advanced diagnostic tools for probing such processes.

Does CO₂ Dissociate on Low-Index Copper Surfaces?

Figure: Schematic of molecule–surface interaction during scattering and dissociation.
This work investigates whether CO₂ can directly dissociate on low-index Cu surfaces under ultra-high vacuum conditions. The study focuses on the dependence of reaction probability on incident energy, molecular orientation, and vibrational excitation.

These findings are highly relevant for heterogeneous catalysis and CO₂ activation, key challenges in mitigating global warming. The results indicate a direct dissociation mechanism with minimal surface temperature dependence.

Development of Diagnostic Techniques and Methods

  • Wavemeter: Developed a low-cost, high-precision wavemeter based on a Fizeau interferometer.
  • Ultra-stable laser: Built a ppb-stabilized He–Ne laser using feedback electronics.
  • Transfer interferometer: Demonstrated MHz-level laser stabilization.
  • Spectrometer: Designed a compact, high-sensitivity spectrometer for wavelength and Raman measurements.
  • Photoacoustic spectroscopy: Developed high-sensitivity detection for weak transitions.
  • Imaging tools: Designed velocity-map and spatial imaging systems for scattering studies.
  • Helium source: Built a compact, high-flux beam for real-time surface monitoring.
